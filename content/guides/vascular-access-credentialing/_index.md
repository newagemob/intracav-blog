# Vascular Access Credentialing and Certification: Complete Guide

Vascular access procedures carry inherent patient safety risks — infection, thrombosis, nerve injury, air embolism, and mechanical complications that depend heavily on operator skill, knowledge, and adherence to evidence-based protocols. Credentialing and certification are the mechanisms by which individual clinicians demonstrate competence and institutions verify that their workforce is qualified to perform these procedures safely.

This guide covers the full credentialing and certification landscape for vascular access professionals: national certification pathways (VA-BC, CRNI), institutional competency frameworks, ultrasound credentialing, scope of practice considerations, and how to build a comprehensive credentialing program.

---

## Why Credentialing Matters

**Patient safety:** Credentialing ensures that clinicians who perform PICC insertions, port accesses, and ultrasound-guided procedures have demonstrated the knowledge and skills required to do so safely. Uncredentialed or inadequately trained inserters are associated with higher complication rates.

**Regulatory requirements:** The Joint Commission's credentialing and privileging standards require hospitals to verify practitioner competency before granting procedure privileges. INS 2021 Standard 13 (Competency and Competency Validation) requires documented initial and ongoing competency for all vascular access procedures.

**Scope of practice:** State nurse practice acts define the scope within which RNs may perform vascular access procedures. Hospital policy further defines specific credentialing requirements. Clinicians operating outside their credentialed scope carry personal and institutional liability.

**Quality outcomes:** Studies consistently show that credentialed vascular access teams — with formal competency validation and ongoing quality review — achieve significantly better clinical outcomes than units where vascular access is performed by uncredentialed general staff.

## Professional Organizations

### AVAR — Association for Vascular Access

The **Association for Vascular Access (AVAR)** is the primary professional organization for vascular access specialists in the United States. AVAR administers the **VA-BC (Vascular Access Board Certified)** credential through its certification board (**CBVN — Certification Board for Vascular Nursing**). AVAR sets vascular access practice standards, provides continuing education, and advocates for the vascular access specialty at the national level.

### INS — Infusion Nurses Society

The **Infusion Nurses Society (INS)** is the professional organization for infusion therapy nurses. INS publishes the **Infusion Therapy Standards of Practice** (the primary clinical practice standard for the specialty) and administers the **CRNI (Certified Registered Nurse Infusion)** credential through the INS Certification Corporation (INCC). INS provides continuing education, an annual conference, and supports evidence-based practice in infusion therapy and vascular access.

## VA-BC Certification

The **Vascular Access Board Certified (VA-BC)** credential is the specialty certification for vascular access professionals, administered by CBVN through AVAR.

### Eligibility Requirements

- Current RN, LPN, or allied health licensure (e.g., RT, PA with vascular access practice)
- Minimum **1,500 hours** of vascular access clinical practice in the preceding 3 years
- Documentation of vascular access-specific procedures (PICC insertions, port accesses, difficult IV placements, etc.)
- No disciplinary action on current license

### Examination Content Areas

The VA-BC exam covers the full scope of vascular access practice. Major content domains (approximate weighting):

1. Patient and procedure assessment (~20%)
2. Device selection and planning (~15%)
3. Insertion techniques and technology (~20%)
4. Device maintenance and management (~20%)
5. Complication prevention and management (~15%)
6. Patient education and professional practice (~10%)

### Application and Exam Process

1. Complete CBVN application with documentation of clinical hours and procedures
2. Submit application fee
3. Receive eligibility notification and schedule exam (computer-based testing at authorized centers)
4. 150-question multiple-choice exam; 3-hour time limit
5. Results typically available immediately or within a few weeks

### Maintaining VA-BC Certification

The VA-BC credential is valid for **3 years** from the date of certification. Renewal requires:
- Current licensure
- Minimum **1,500 clinical hours** in vascular access during the certification period
- **40 contact hours** of continuing education in vascular access
- OR re-examination

## CRNI Certification

The **Certified Registered Nurse Infusion (CRNI)** credential is administered by the INCC (INS Certification Corporation) and is the specialty certification for infusion therapy nursing, with significant overlap with vascular access practice.

### Eligibility Requirements

- Current RN licensure
- Minimum **1,600 hours** of infusion therapy nursing practice in the preceding 2 years (at least 800 hours in direct patient care)

### Examination Content Areas

The CRNI exam covers:
1. Vascular access (~30%)
2. Patient care (~25%)
3. Fluid and electrolytes (~15%)
4. Pharmacology (~15%)
5. Infection prevention (~15%)

### CRNI vs. VA-BC: Which to Pursue?

| Feature | VA-BC | CRNI |
|---|---|---|
| Focus | Vascular access specialty | Infusion therapy + vascular access |
| Certifying body | CBVN/AVAR | INCC/INS |
| Exam emphasis | Device-specific, procedural | Broader infusion therapy scope |
| Best for | VAT clinicians, PICC nurses, IV team specialists | Infusion therapy nurses with broad practice scope |

Many VA specialists hold both credentials, though pursuing one first and maintaining both requires ongoing CE investment. Both are recognized by TJC and CMS as indicators of specialty competency.

## Institutional Credentialing Frameworks

### PICC Insertion Credentialing

PICC insertion is a high-complexity procedural skill requiring structured initial training, supervised practice, and ongoing competency verification. Elements of a robust institutional credentialing framework:

**Initial credentialing pathway:**
1. Didactic training: minimum 8 hours covering anatomy, indications, insertion technique, CEVAD standards, complications, documentation
2. Simulation: minimum 2 proctored PICC insertions on task trainer/phantom model
3. Proctored clinical cases: minimum 5 successful live PICC insertions with licensed proctor attestation for each
4. Written competency examination: ≥85% passing score

**Ongoing competency:**
- Minimum **12 PICCs per year** to maintain independent practice status; OR 2 directly observed insertions annually
- Annual review of any complication events involving the credentialed clinician
- Annual didactic update or self-assessment activity

### Ultrasound Credentialing

See [Ultrasound Credentialing Requirements](/guides/ultrasound-guided-vascular-access/ultrasound-credentialing-requirements/) for the full framework. In brief:

- Didactic training: US physics, anatomy, vein vs. artery identification, short-axis and long-axis technique
- Supervised US-guided insertions: minimum 5 for US-PIV; the PICC insertion competency already incorporates US guidance
- Annual competency verification

### Implanted Port Access Credentialing

- Shorter training pathway than PICC
- Written exam covering port anatomy, Huber needle selection, aseptic technique, patency assessment, deaccessing
- 3 supervised port accesses (simulation accepted for 1 of 3)
- Annual verification

### Institutional Privileging (Medical Staff)

For procedures requiring medical staff privileges (non-tunneled CVC, arterial line, tunneled catheter placement):

- Privileges are granted by the medical staff office based on credentialing application, training verification, and ongoing performance evaluation (OPPE/FPPE)
- Nurses do not receive medical staff "privileges" per se — their scope is governed by state NPA and hospital policy/credentialing
- NPs and PAs performing invasive vascular access procedures (PICC, US-guided CVC) require appropriate privileging through the medical staff process

See [Institutional Privileging for Vascular Access](/guides/vascular-access-credentialing/institutional-privileging-framework/).

## Competency Validation Methods

INS 2021 and AVAR recognize several competency validation approaches:

| Method | Description | Best Used For |
|---|---|---|
| Written examination | Knowledge test covering required content areas | Initial and annual theoretical competency |
| Skills checklist observation | Direct observation of procedural skills by credentialed assessor | PICC, port access, US-guided PIV |
| Case log review | Documentation of procedure volume and outcomes over defined period | Ongoing competency for volume-dependent skills |
| Simulation | Manikin, task trainer, or VR-based procedure practice | Initial training and remediation |
| Annual skills fair | Structured station-based competency day | Efficient annual re-verification across a large workforce |

**Simulation in vascular access training:**

Simulation allows skill development without patient risk and is an essential component of initial PICC insertion training. Available modalities include:
- Arm phantom models (static or with simulated blood return)
- Ultrasound training phantoms (gelatin/tissue-equivalent models for US-guided insertion practice)
- Full-body simulation manikins (for CVC and full-body scenario-based training)
- Virtual reality (VR) simulation platforms (emerging; currently limited vascular access-specific content)

## Scope of Practice Considerations

**State nurse practice acts (NPAs)**: RN scope of practice varies by state. In most states, PICC insertion is within RN scope of practice when the nurse has received appropriate training and is practicing under institutional policy and physician order. Some states require specific designation or standing orders for PICC insertion by RNs.

**INS position on PICC insertion**: INS supports PICC insertion by RNs with documented competency, appropriate training, and institutional authorization. INS does not support PICC insertion as an advanced practice-only function.

**Advanced practice roles**: NPs and PAs with appropriate training and privileges may insert PICCs, non-tunneled CVCs, and perform other advanced vascular access procedures within their scope.

## Building an Institutional Credentialing Program

**Step-by-step:**

1. **Needs assessment**: Identify which vascular access procedures are performed by which clinician roles; identify current credentialing gaps
2. **Policy development**: Develop or update the vascular access credentialing policy aligned with INS 2021, AVAR, and TJC standards
3. **Competency tools**: Select or develop written exam, skills checklist, and case log template for each procedure module
4. **Training program**: Identify didactic resources (INS courses, AVAR modules, institutional content), simulation resources, and preceptor/proctor pool
5. **Documentation system**: Implement credentialing records in a competency management platform or personnel file system
6. **Launch and orientation integration**: Integrate vascular access credentialing into new hire orientation and annual competency programs
7. **Ongoing monitoring**: Track competency completion rates, procedure volumes, and quality outcomes; review annually
